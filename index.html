Quantum Information Science I & II
MIT Open Learning Library · Peter Shor + Isaac Chuang · 6 courses · Free · No registration
These 6 MIT courses — taught by Peter Shor (inventor of Shor's algorithm) and Isaac Chuang (co-author of the definitive QC textbook) — are the definitive free quantum computing curriculum in the world. They are hosted on the MIT Open Learning Library you shared. Do all 6 in order: QIS I (Parts 1→2→3), then QIS II (Parts 1→2→3).
🔑 This is the quantum computing core. After Stage 3 you will understand: quantum circuits, Grover's algorithm, Shor's algorithm, quantum teleportation, quantum error correction, fault tolerance, and quantum complexity. This is taught by the people who invented these things.

Phase 1 — Mathematical & Classical Foundations
~5 months · Do before any quantum content
These MIT courses build the mathematical vocabulary quantum mechanics is written in. Linear algebra and differential equations are not optional prerequisites — they are the language itself. Complete Phase 1 before touching any Phase 2 course.
⭐ Do First — Non-Negotiable
18.06 · Linear Algebra
Prof. Gilbert Strang · MIT OCW Spring 2010 · Free
Vector spaces, linear transformations, eigenvalues and eigenvectors, orthogonality, inner products, singular value decomposition. Quantum mechanics IS linear algebra — a quantum state is a vector in Hilbert space, a quantum gate is a unitary matrix, a measurement is an eigenvalue problem. This course is the single most important prerequisite for all of quantum computing.
Vector spacesEigenvaluesEigenvectorsInner productsUnitary matricesSVDProjections

Phase 2 — Quantum Mechanics I → II → III: The Shared Core
~10 months · Compulsory for both tracks
The MIT 8.04 → 8.05 → 8.06 sequence is the world's best undergraduate QM curriculum — free on OCW with complete lecture notes, problem sets, and solutions. These courses form the basis of the MIT Press textbook "Mastering Quantum Mechanics" (Zwiebach, 2022). The chemistry track runs 5.61 → 5.73 in parallel. Both pathways converge at the end of Phase 2.
⭐ Both Tracks — Start Here — World's Best Free QM Course
8.04 · Quantum Physics I — Wave Mechanics & Schrödinger Equation
Prof. Barton Zwiebach (2016) + Prof. Allan Adams (2013) · MIT OCW · Free with full notes
Historical development of quantum theory, de Broglie waves, uncertainty principle, Schrödinger equation in 1D and 3D, particle in a box, quantum harmonic oscillator with ladder operators, hydrogen atom. Two versions available: Adams (2013) is more intuitive and conceptual; Zwiebach (2016) is more rigorous and systematic. Use both — Adams first for intuition, Zwiebach for depth.

8.05 · Quantum Physics II — Dirac Notation & Formal Quantum Mechanics
Prof. Barton Zwiebach · MIT OCW Fall 2013 · Free with full notes
Dirac bra-ket notation, Hilbert spaces, Hermitian operators, commutators, quantum dynamics (Schrödinger/Heisenberg/Dirac pictures), spin-½ and Pauli matrices, addition of angular momenta and Clebsch-Gordan coefficients, identical particles and symmetrization, time-independent perturbation theory, WKB approximation. This is the rigorous mathematical foundation required for all of quantum computing — qubits live in the Hilbert space formalism taught here.

8.06 · Quantum Physics III — Modern Applications
Prof. Barton Zwiebach / Prof. Aram Harrow · MIT OCW 2016/2018 · Free
Time-dependent perturbation theory, Fermi's golden rule, sudden and adiabatic approximations, fine and hyperfine structure of hydrogen, Zeeman effect, Landau levels (quantum Hall), scattering theory (Born approximation, partial waves), Aharonov-Bohm effect, lasers and stimulated emission. ⭐ SW Research · Scott Aaronson · After QIS II
6.845 · Quantum Complexity Theory
Prof. Scott Aaronson · MIT OCW Fall 2010 · Free with full lecture notes
The rigorous mathematical theory of quantum computational complexity — what quantum computers can and cannot do efficiently, and why. BQP vs P/BPP, oracle separations (BQP ≠ P relative to random oracle), polynomial method for quantum lower bounds, Ambainis adversary method, quantum interactive proofs (QIP = PSPACE), quantum communication complexity, quantum proofs and advice. Essential for anyone working on quantum algorithms research — you need to know whether a proposed quantum speedup is real or could be dequantized.
